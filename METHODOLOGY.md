# MetaJudge — Development Methodology

How the benchmark was designed, built, and validated. This document describes the intellectual arc, not the engineering iteration. For the theoretical framework, see `docs/theoretical_backgrounder.md`. For per-family design rationale, see the literature reports and scoring specs in `docs/`.

---

## Phase 1: Domain Survey

The project began with a systematic review of the metacognition literature, tracing the construct from its origins in developmental psychology (Flavell, 1979; Hart, 1965) through the metamemory framework of Nelson & Narens (1990) to the current LLM evaluation frontier (Huang et al., ICLR 2024; Tyen et al., 2024; Kadavath et al., 2022).

Nelson & Narens' two-process model — **monitoring** (assessing cognitive state) and **control** (acting on that assessment) — provided the architectural foundation. Every metacognitive behavior is either monitoring or control. This distinction is not a design choice; it is the field's consensus taxonomy, and it determines how MetaJudge is structured.

The literature review surfaced a critical gap: most existing LLM evaluations of metacognition rely on self-report (asking the model how confident it is) rather than behavioral evidence (observing whether the model's actions change based on its uncertainty). The Kaggle competition's assessor recommendations explicitly flagged this weakness, recommending that benchmarks "score metacognition by the consequences of self-monitoring on task behavior, not by what the model says about itself."

This principle became MetaJudge's design axiom.

See: `docs/metacognition_literature_report.md` (65+ papers), `docs/metacognition_assessor_recommendations.md`

## Phase 2: Family Design

Three measurement families were designed, each targeting a specific metacognitive process:

**Family A — Confidence Calibration (Monitoring).** The simplest operationalization: ask a question, get an answer and a confidence score, check whether confidence tracks accuracy. Scored with the Brier rule (Brier, 1950), which is strictly proper — it cannot be gamed by systematic over- or under-confidence. This family tests pure monitoring with no action decision.

**Family B — Selective Abstention (Control).** Grounded in the report/withhold paradigm (Koriat & Goldsmith, 1996): given uncertainty, does the model choose the right epistemic action? Four possible actions — answer, clarify, verify, abstain — scored via a utility matrix that rewards correct action selection and penalizes confident wrong answers more heavily than cautious abstention. This family tests whether monitoring signals are translated into appropriate control decisions.

**Family C — Self-Correction (Control).** The most theoretically complex family. Two subfamilies test different aspects of the monitoring→control pipeline:
- **C1 (Intrinsic):** Model prompted to reconsider with no new evidence. Tests whether internal error detection drives revision. Huang et al. (ICLR 2024) showed this rarely improves performance — a finding our results confirm.
- **C2 (Evidence-assisted):** Model given external evidence that may be accurate, misleading, or irrelevant. Tests whether the model can appropriately integrate or reject new information.

The C1/C2 split is not arbitrary. Tyen et al. (2024) demonstrated that the bottleneck in self-correction is detection (a monitoring process), not repair (a control process). C1 tests the full monitoring→control pipeline where detection is the binding constraint. C2 relaxes the detection constraint by providing external evidence, isolating the control component.

## Phase 3: Item Development

### The saturation problem

Early item development used publicly available question sets (trivia, math, science). These saturated quickly — frontier models answered correctly with high confidence, leaving no room for metacognitive differentiation. LLM-generated adversarial items (using one model to fool another) also saturated within a few development cycles.

### Multi-agent orchestrated generation

The solution was a multi-stage pipeline that combined deep domain research with automated adversarial hardening:

1. **Domain research.** Per-family literature surveys identified specific cognitive traps — base-rate neglect, percentage asymmetry, counterintuitive true facts, false-presupposition framing — that reliably produce metacognitive errors in both humans and LLMs.

2. **Author→Adversary→Canary→Frontier.** Items were generated by one model (author), stress-tested by another (adversary), piloted on canary models to establish difficulty, and validated against frontier models to confirm they were neither too easy nor too hard. This pipeline used escalating model pairings: weaker models for initial generation, stronger models for adversarial review.

3. **Iterative hardening.** Items that all pilot models answered correctly were quarantined (too easy for wrong-to-right strata). Items that no models answered correctly were excluded (ungradeable). The surviving items occupy the productive middle — difficult enough to reveal metacognitive differences, not so difficult that all models fail uniformly.

### Contamination control

All calibration and abstention items were hand-authored or generated with specific adversarial mechanisms, not drawn from existing datasets. Family C items use novel question-evidence pairings that cannot appear in training data. The grading engine uses a registry of accepted answer forms per item, not string matching, reducing the chance of spurious correctness from memorized phrasing.

## Phase 4: Scoring Architecture

### Per-family metrics

Each family uses a metric suited to its construct:

- **Brier score** for calibration — the only strictly proper scoring rule that jointly rewards accuracy and calibration. A model that knows what it knows scores higher than one that is always confident.
- **UWAA** (Utility-Weighted Action Accuracy) for abstention — maps each (model action × gold action) pair to a utility in [−1, +1] via a payoff matrix. Corrective responses on false-presupposition items receive partial credit. Acceptable alternative actions (e.g., verify when the gold is clarify) receive reduced credit rather than zero.
- **Transition-based scoring** for self-correction — classifies each item into one of six outcomes (correction_gain, maintain_correct, neutral_revision, damage, stubborn_wrong, failed_revision) with config-driven weights. The damage:gain asymmetry reflects the real-world cost structure: introducing new errors is worse than failing to fix existing ones.

### Composite MetaScore

The four task scores are anchor-normalized to [0, 1] using floor/ceiling values derived from the pilot sweep, then averaged with equal weights. Equal weighting is not a default — it is provably optimal at small n. Dawes (1979) and Davis-Stober (2011) showed that with k components and n observations, any data-derived differential weighting will have higher expected error than equal weights when n is small relative to k.

### Deterministic grading

All answer grading is performed by an 8-rule deterministic engine (`grading_v2.py`) using a per-item registry of accepted forms. Rules include tolerance-aware numeric comparison, alias matching, tri-label classification, code output evaluation, and fraction parsing. No LLM judge is used anywhere in the scoring pipeline. This makes every score exactly reproducible.

## Phase 5: Validation

### Grading accuracy

A two-layer audit ensures the deterministic grader produces correct results:

1. **Automated re-grading.** Every item is re-graded using the production engine and compared to the original results. Correctness flips are flagged and investigated. Seven grading failure patterns have been identified and fixed across prior audits (verbose answers, confirmation-without-restatement, numeric tolerance, LaTeX notation, first-number extraction, comma handling, markdown formatting).

2. **LLM semantic audit.** A long-context LLM reviews all items marked wrong and evaluates whether the model's answer is semantically correct despite failing the automated grader. This catches alias gaps, phrasing variants, and gold answer errors that the rule-based engine misses.

### Reproducibility

Dual-run stochasticity checks (Family B, C1, C2) run each task twice — once cached (scored), once independent (display only). Item-level agreement and score-level spread quantify response variance. The triplicate analysis (5 models × 3 independent runs) during development yielded ICC = 0.758, above the 0.75 threshold for "good" reliability.

### Subscore validity

Family C reliability (Cronbach's α ≈ 0.35) is below the Haberman (2008) threshold of 0.55 for standalone subscore value. The A+B composite predicts true C better than C's observed score. Family C is included in the MetaScore for profile information but should not be interpreted as a standalone axis.

## Design Principles

1. **Behavioral evidence over self-report.** Score what models do, not what they claim about themselves.
2. **Deterministic grading.** No LLM judge. Every score is reproducible.
3. **Asymmetric damage penalties.** Introducing errors is worse than failing to fix them.
4. **Theory-first architecture.** The monitoring/control split comes from the psychology, not from engineering convenience.
5. **Transparent validation.** Every gold answer has a written justification. Every grading rule is in the registry. Every exclusion is documented.
